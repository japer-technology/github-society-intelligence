**Source figure:** [13-6.md](13-6.md) — *A child's drawing of a

**Analysis target:** [`FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/)

This file inventories the ideas carried by Figure 13-6 and checks
each one against the implementation plan.

---

## The ideas Figure 13-6 actually carries

From the caption and §13.3 *Seeing and believing*:

1. **A correct procedure can produce a strange output.** The child
   is not failing — the procedure is succeeding on the description
   it was given.
2. **Output adequacy is judged by the schema, not by the world.**
   If the schema does not demand that head and body be distinct,
   then collapsing them is *correct*.
3. **Behaviour is evidence of representation.** The drawing is
   diagnostic: it shows what the schema must look like inside.
4. **Errors are usually bookkeeping errors, not perception
   errors.** The child can plainly see better-formed bodies; what
   is missing is the rule that requires *another* large closed
   figure.
5. **A schema's silences are doing work.** What the description
   *fails to constrain* shapes the output as strongly as what it
   does constrain.
6. **An external observer can reconstruct the internal schema
   from the artefact.** Figures 13-7 and 13-8 are that
   reconstruction.

## What the implementation already absorbs

### 1 — Outputs are auditable artefacts

[`08-state-and-memory.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/08-state-and-memory.md)
and the workflow pipeline in
[`03-runtime-pipeline.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/03-runtime-pipeline.md)
preserve the signals, settlements, and patches an agency produced,
in git. An observer can therefore read the artefact and try to
reconstruct the schema that produced it. Point 6 has a structural
form.

### 2 — Manifests as inspectable schemas

Every unit's behaviour is governed by a manifest written into the
repo; nothing important about an agency is hidden in code that is
not also in a markdown file. Point 3 (behaviour as evidence of
representation) is honoured because the representation is on
disk.

### 3 — Settle as the rule that accepts outputs

`settle` in
[`03-runtime-pipeline.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/03-runtime-pipeline.md)
decides whether a workspace is ready to act on. It is the
analogue of "the procedure considers itself done" — including the
case where the output is strange but the schema is satisfied.

## What the implementation does not yet take into account

### A — Critics are mostly about evidence, not bookkeeping

`critic.evidence` and `critic.scope` check claims against
artefacts and check whether the unit stayed within bounds. They
do not ask "did the schema you ran require the right things?"
Point 4 (errors are usually bookkeeping errors) suggests a
distinct critic kind — `critic.bookkeeping` — that asks whether
the constraints on the schema are tight enough. No such critic
exists in the first-ship catalogues in
[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md).

### B — Silences in a schema are not surfaced

Point 5 — that what a schema fails to require shapes outputs —
has no operational form. There is no tool that reads a manifest
and reports "this schema does not require the following
distinctions, so outputs may freely conflate them." Schema gaps
are visible only when something downstream goes wrong.

### C — No notion of "the strange output is not an error"

The runtime's vocabulary for an output is *accepted* (`settle`
ok) or *rejected* (critic/censor refusal). There is no third
state for "accepted by the schema, surprising to a maintainer,
diagnostic of an under-specified schema." Figure 13-6's output
needs that third state to be discussed properly.

### D — Reconstructing the schema from outputs is manual

Point 6 is honoured only because a human can read both manifest
and artefacts. There is no automated path that says "given these
recent settlements, infer the schema that would have produced
them." Without it, a too-permissive schema can persist for many
runs before anyone notices.

### E — Schema silences are not versioned as silences

When a manifest is later tightened — adding the missing
constraint — git records the diff, but not "we discovered that
the previous schema was silent on X." The transition from
Figure 13-6 to Figure 13-9 is exactly such a discovery, and the
plan has no schema for representing it as a category of change
distinct from a normal manifest edit.

## Implication for the plan (no changes proposed here)

Figure 13-6 is the figure that earns the distinction *correct
under the schema, wrong by intent*. The implementation plan
honours auditability and explicit schemas, but does not yet
honour the diagnostic value of strange-but-accepted outputs.

The gaps above are recorded here as analysis, not as a change
request. Any move to close them — for example, a
`critic.bookkeeping`, a "schema silence" report, or a new
settlement kind for under-specification discoveries — would
touch the catalogues in
[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md),
[`07-policies-and-safety.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/07-policies-and-safety.md),
and the identity scopes in
`THE-SOCIETY-OF-REPO/02-protocols/01-identity.md`, and so falls
under the "stop and ask" rules in
[`AGENTS.md`](../../../../../AGENTS.md) §12 and
[`CLAUDE.md`](../../../../../CLAUDE.md).
