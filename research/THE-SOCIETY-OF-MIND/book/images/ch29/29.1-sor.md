# Figure 29-1 — Implementation analysis (SOR / FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION)

**Source figure:** [29-1.md](29-1.md) — *Epigraph from Allen Newell
(the realms of thought).*
**Analysis target:** [`FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/)
(plan-of-record for `.forgejo-society/` and
`.forgejo/workflows/forgejo-society.yaml`).

Figure 29-1 is an epigraph, not a mechanism. The material it carries
is a *posture* — that several views of the mind coexist, ask their
own questions, and rarely translate cleanly into one another. This
file checks which parts of that posture the implementation plan
already embodies and which it leaves implicit.

---

## The ideas Figure 29-1 actually carries

1. **Multiple views are the working condition.** No single realm
   ("physiology", "psychology", "computation") owns the description.
2. **Each view brings its own questions.** Vocabulary, level of
   detail, and admissible answers differ across realms.
3. **Views mostly speak past each other.** Translation is the
   exception, not the rule.
4. **Occasional comparison is possible — but not on demand.**
   Cross-realm alignment must be earned, not assumed.
5. **A working theory of mind tolerates plurality.** Coexistence,
   not unification, is the goal.
6. **The chapter is opening a discussion of realms, not closing
   one.** The epigraph sets a stance the rest of Chapter 29 will
   operationalise (encyclopedia pages, Trans-frames, paranomes).

---

## What the implementation already absorbs

### 1 — Plural surfaces, plural authors

[`00-overview.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/00-overview.md)
treats the runtime as a collection of *agencies* with distinct
competences (perception, memory, code, safety, identity, integration,
assembly, meta-admin) rather than a single model that "thinks." Each
family asks its own questions: `agency.perception.*` asks "what
arrived?", `agency.code.*` asks "what would the change be?",
`agency.safety.*` asks "is this allowed?". The plan inherits the
posture of Figure 29-1: many partial views.

### 2 — No forced translation between realms

[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md)
keeps critic outputs and censor outputs typed separately. A safety
verdict does not become a code suggestion; an evidence critic does
not become a settlement. The plan does not require any single agency
to render every other agency's view in its own terms — which matches
Newell's "speak mostly past each other."

### 3 — Comparison is an explicit, named event

When realms must be compared, the plan uses `settle`
([`03-runtime-pipeline.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/03-runtime-pipeline.md)
and [`08-state-and-memory.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/08-state-and-memory.md))
to produce a settlement that *quotes* findings from multiple agencies
rather than fusing them. This mirrors the epigraph's "comparison is
possible, but not often and not on demand": settlement is a discrete,
auditable moment, not a continuous translation layer.

---

## What the implementation does not yet take into account

### A — *Realm* has no first-class representation

The plan groups units into families and uses scopes like `agency`,
`critic`, `censor`, `memory`, `workspace`. None of these is a *realm*
in Newell's sense — a *style of questioning* that cuts across
families. There is no field that marks an agency as "operating in the
physical realm" vs "the possessional realm" vs "the mental realm",
and no identifier scope in
`THE-SOCIETY-OF-REPO/02-protocols/01-identity.md` for it. The
epigraph's organising idea is therefore not addressable.

### B — No declared limit on translation

The plan offers no place to record "agency X's findings are not
directly commensurate with agency Y's findings, and any cross-use
must pass through Z." Newell's caution that views *speak past each
other* is honoured by accident (typed outputs) but not by policy.
A future censor of the form `censor.realm-crossing` is not currently
named in
[`07-policies-and-safety.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/07-policies-and-safety.md).

### C — No "stance" surface

Figure 29-1 is itself a *stance* the chapter takes before any
mechanism is shown. The plan has manifests, schemas, and workflows,
but no surface for *epistemic posture* — no document a manifest can
point at that says "this unit assumes plurality of realms and does
not attempt unification." The closest analogue is prose in
[`00-overview.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/00-overview.md),
which is read by humans, not by the runtime.

### D — Chapter-opening epigraphs carry no operational analogue

Honest note: an epigraph is not a diagram of parts. Most of what
Figure 29-1 "carries" is tone-setting for figures 29-2 through 29-6.
The plan does not, and arguably should not, try to implement an
epigraph. The real implementation tests for this chapter live in the
analyses of [29-2](29-2.md), [29-3](29-3.md), [29-4](29-4.md),
[29-5](29-5.md), and especially [29-6](29-6.md).

## Implication for the plan (no changes proposed here)

Figure 29-1 records a stance the implementation already lives, but
does not name. Promoting "realm" to a first-class concept — a new
identifier scope, a `realm:` field on manifests, or a
`censor.realm-crossing` — would touch the identity protocol in
`THE-SOCIETY-OF-REPO/02-protocols/01-identity.md` and the manifest
schema in
[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md),
which falls under the "stop and ask" rules in
[`AGENTS.md`](../../../../../AGENTS.md) §12 and
[`CLAUDE.md`](../../../../../CLAUDE.md). Nothing is proposed here.
