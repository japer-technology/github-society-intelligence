# Figure 27-1 — Implementation analysis (SOR / FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION)

**Source figure:** [27-1.md](27-1.md) — *Epigraph from Sigmund Freud
(mayonnaise-for-spinach).*
**Analysis target:** [`FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/)
(plan-of-record for `.forgejo-society/` and
`.forgejo/workflows/forgejo-society.yaml`).

Figure 27-1 is an epigraph, not a diagram. The ideas it carries are
those Minsky uses it to license at the head of the chapter on
*Censors and Jokes*. This file inventories those ideas and checks
each against the implementation plan.

---

## The ideas Figure 27-1 actually carries

1. **The wrong rule fired.** The diner's mistake is not the missing
   condiment but the unexamined rule *spinach belongs in hair*.
2. **The right rule did not fire in time.** Whatever should have
   intercepted *do not put food in your hair* never reached the
   action.
3. **The apology is itself diagnostic.** What the agent says
   afterwards reveals which censor it thinks it broke.
4. **A late catch is still a catch, but a costly one.** The error
   reaches an audience before any correction occurs.
5. **Two failure modes live in the same story.** A suppressor that
   should have blocked the act, and a censor that should have blocked
   the state of mind that preceded it.
6. **Humour is the side effect of a near-miss.** The chapter uses
   the laugh as a *signal* that a suppressor or censor fired
   unusually, not as content in its own right.
7. **Self-explanation is unreliable.** The diner's account of why he
   acted is plausible and wrong; the actual cause is a missing
   intercept, not the proffered reason.
8. **Epigraphs frame what counts as evidence.** Minsky cites Freud
   rather than a cognitive experiment; the chapter treats lived
   anecdote as legitimate input for theorising about intercepts.

## What the implementation already absorbs

### 1 — Censors and suppressors as distinct, named things

[`07-policies-and-safety.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/07-policies-and-safety.md)
names *censors* and *suppressors* as separate first-ship layers, with
distinct files (`censors/*.md`, suppressor blocks inside
`policies/danger-zones.yml`). The conceptual distinction the epigraph
turns on is therefore present in the schema, not only in the prose.

### 2 — Late catches are still recorded

A blocked outcome is logged as fully as a successful one: censor
blocks go to `signals.jsonl` with `censor.<id>.blocked`, suppressor
activations to `objections.jsonl`, approval-gate redirections to
`workspace/owner-briefings/<settlement_id>.md`. The "I'm so sorry"
moment has an operational analogue — every interception leaves a
trace, including the ones that fired only at the last step.

### 3 — Self-report is not trusted alone

The plan does not let an agency's own justification overrule
mechanical checks. `policy.ts` computes the allowed tool surface
deterministically; a model's account of *why* it tried to use a
denied tool is recorded but does not change the verdict. This matches
the epigraph's reading: the diner's explanation is preserved, but the
intercept does not depend on it.

## What the implementation does not yet take into account

### A — No "near-miss" signal class

The epigraph's whole interest is in the laugh — the marker that an
intercept fired unusually. The plan logs `blocked` and `denied`
events but has no signal kind for *almost*: a candidate that passed
every censor but only by a thin margin, or one that triggered a
suppressor that has rarely fired before. There is no
`signal.kind: near-miss` and no field on a censor verdict that
records how close the call was.

### B — No record of which rule the agent *thought* it was following

When a censor blocks, the runtime records the censor's verdict, not
the agent's stated intent. The Freud anecdote is funny because the
two diverge: the diner's apology reveals one rule, the act reveals
another. The plan has no slot in the settlement for "the agency
believed it was acting under rule X" alongside "the censor blocked
under rule Y," and so cannot surface the *divergence* between stated
and effective rule.

### C — No catalogue of historically learned suppressors

Minsky's suppressors are *learned* — they come from past mistakes.
The first-ship suppressor list in
[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md)
and the danger-zones in
[`07-policies-and-safety.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/07-policies-and-safety.md)
are *designed*, not derived from prior failed runs. There is no
mechanism that inspects
`memory/failure/rejected-candidates/`
([`08-state-and-memory.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/08-state-and-memory.md))
and proposes new suppressor blocks from recurring patterns.

### D — No humour-as-diagnostic channel

The chapter treats laughter as a useful signal about the structure
of one's own censors. The plan has no equivalent diagnostic surface
that highlights "this run is interesting because intercept X fired
unusually." `report` produces a settlement; it does not flag
*surprising* settlements for human attention separately from
*blocked* ones.

### E — Epigraphic / anecdotal evidence has no home

The plan accepts code, diffs, signals, and structured memory.
Anecdotal evidence — "a user told us this almost went wrong" — has
no manifest form, no ingest path, and no place in
`memory/`. Minsky's willingness to take a Freud story as evidence
has no operational analogue.

## Implication for the plan (no changes proposed here)

Figure 27-1 is the figure that licenses the rest of the chapter by
insisting that *near-misses are data*. The plan honours the binary —
blocked vs allowed — but does not yet honour the gradient that the
epigraph turns on.

Any move to close the gaps above — a `near-miss` signal kind, an
`agent_stated_rule` field on verdicts, a learner that proposes
suppressors from `memory/failure/`, or an anecdote ingest path —
would touch the signal schema in
[`09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md),
the censor and suppressor catalogues in
[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md)
and
[`07-policies-and-safety.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/07-policies-and-safety.md),
and the memory layout in
[`08-state-and-memory.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/08-state-and-memory.md),
and so falls under the "stop and ask" rules in
[`AGENTS.md`](../../../../../AGENTS.md) §12 and
[`CLAUDE.md`](../../../../../CLAUDE.md).
