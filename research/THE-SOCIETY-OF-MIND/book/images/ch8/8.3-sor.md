# Figure 8-3 — Implementation analysis (SOR / FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION)

**Source figure:** [8-3.md](8-3.md) — *Three policies for handling K-line conflict.*
**Analysis target:** [`FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/)
(plan-of-record for `.forgejo-society/` and
`.forgejo/workflows/forgejo-society.yaml`).

This file inventories the ideas carried by Figure 8-3 and checks each
one against the implementation plan.

---

## The ideas Figure 8-3 actually carries

1. **K-line conflict is normal and must be settled by a named
   policy.** Implicit merging is not enough.
2. **Three canonical policies exist:** old wins, new wins, and
   non-compromise (mutual veto).
3. **Each policy has a different information cost.** Old-wins
   overwrites current work; new-wins discards memory; non-compromise
   loses both sides except where they agree.
4. **"Non-compromise" is a productive outcome, not a failure mode.**
   It surfaces exactly the agents on which the two patterns agree —
   that intersection is itself the answer.
5. **The choice of policy is contextual.** Different tasks deserve
   different schemes; the policy is not a fixed property of the
   system.
6. **Conflict resolution is *cognition*, not infrastructure.** The
   figure treats it as a thinking act, not as a plumbing detail.
7. **The contested slot is the unit of resolution.** Settlement
   happens per agent (per cell), not per K-line as a whole.

---

## What the implementation already absorbs

### 1 — Settlement as an explicit named act

The plan has a `settle` phase, a settlement schema with required
slots, and a settlement record per stimulus
([`09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md)).
Resolution is *named*; it is not implicit averaging.

### 2 — Graduated inhibition shapes the field

`criticize` (tail) updates `activation.jsonl` from sustained
objections ([`03-runtime-pipeline.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/03-runtime-pipeline.md)).
This is one form of "lose to the critic" — analogous to new-wins
when the critic backs the current task over a prior pattern.

### 3 — Inhibitory signals on manifests

`inhibits:` on agency manifests
([`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md))
lets one unit suppress another. This gives the *mechanism* for any
of the three policies, even though no policy uses it that way today.

### 4 — Critics arbitrate proposal-level disputes

Critics can sustain objections that block or weaken candidate
actions. This is *some* form of arbitration — though over proposals,
not over the underlying activation conflict.

---

## What the implementation does not yet take into account

### A — No "K-line conflict" primitive

Settlement resolves *proposal* conflicts (candidate actions, critic
objections), not *activation* conflicts between two K-lines pulling
the same agency in opposite directions. The schema has no
`kline_conflicts:` block recording "agency X received contradictory
boosts from kP and kQ; resolution = …."

### B — No taxonomy of resolution policies

There is no `kline-conflict-policy: old-wins | new-wins |
non-compromise` knob anywhere — not on the frame, not on the
agency, not on the policy ledger. The three named policies of
Figure 8-3 have no operational handle.

### C — Non-compromise has no analogue

"Silence the contested cells, keep only what both patterns agree on"
is structurally absent. Activation weights combine; they do not
mutually annihilate. The plan offers no mechanism by which two
boosts on the same agency, if disagreeing in *direction* or *role*,
quench each other.

### D — Per-cell resolution does not exist

Settlement operates over proposals and frame slots, not over
individual agency activations. Figure 8-3's "this cell is contested,
that cell is uncontested" distinction has no representation in the
settlement record.

### E — Contextual policy choice is absent

Even if the three policies existed, the plan has no place that says
"under the `bug` frame use old-wins; under the `novel` frame use
non-compromise." Frame manifests carry `default_critics:` and
`default_censors:`, not a default K-line conflict policy.

### F — The figure's *information cost* observation is unrepresented

The figure makes the trade-off explicit: each policy *loses* something
specific. The plan has no record of what was lost when conflicting
K-lines were combined — credit assignment notes which K-lines were
reactivated, not which K-line's contribution was suppressed by
another's.

## Implication for the plan (no changes proposed here)

Figure 8-3 is the figure that turns K-lines from a memory mechanism
into a *political* mechanism — multiple memories with a stake in the
same agent must be settled. The plan has the vocabulary of
settlement, but applies it one level up (proposals) rather than at
the K-line/activation level the figure describes. Closing the gaps
would touch the activation protocol in
[`06-frames-polynemes-klines.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/06-frames-polynemes-klines.md),
the settlement schema in
[`09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md),
and the policy ledger noted in
[`07-policies-and-safety.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/07-policies-and-safety.md),
and so falls under the "stop and ask" rules in
[`AGENTS.md`](../../../../../AGENTS.md) §12 and
[`CLAUDE.md`](../../../../../CLAUDE.md).
