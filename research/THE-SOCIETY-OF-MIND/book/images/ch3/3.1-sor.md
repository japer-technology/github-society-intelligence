# Figure 3-1 — Implementation analysis (SOR / FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION)

**Source figure:** [3-1.md](3-1.md) — *PLAY, with BUILDER and WRECKER
as rivals.*
**Analysis target:** [`FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/)
(plan-of-record for `.forgejo-society/` and
`.forgejo/workflows/forgejo-society.yaml`).

This file inventories the ideas carried by Figure 3-1 and checks each
one against the implementation plan. The aim is to mark, plainly, what
the plan already absorbs and what it does not yet name.

---

## The ideas Figure 3-1 actually carries

Pulled from the figure caption and Minsky's surrounding text in §3.1
*Conflict*:

1. **Conflict is the default condition of a mind.** Not an exception,
   not a failure mode — the standard shape of every layer.
2. **Conflict appears at every level of the hierarchy.** EAT vs PLAY
   vs SLEEP at the top, PLAY-WITH-DOLLS vs PLAY-WITH-BLOCKS vs
   PLAY-WITH-ANIMALS one level down, BUILDER vs WRECKER inside
   PLAY-WITH-BLOCKS.
3. **Top-level agencies compete for a shared resource.** EAT, PLAY and
   SLEEP cannot all run the body at once; whichever wins gets the
   limbs for a while.
4. **Sibling agencies are rivals by construction.** Two children of
   the same parent want the same hands and the same minutes and so
   stand in permanent opposition.
5. **A constructive agency and a destructive agency share a parent.**
   BUILDER and WRECKER are both legitimate children of
   PLAY-WITH-BLOCKS; the design admits destruction as a first-class
   citizen rather than treating it as malfunction.
6. **The arbiter is structural, not deliberative.** The figure does
   not show a referee agency. The conflict is resolved by whichever
   agency is currently *louder* (more activated), and the loser is
   inhibited until the next round.
7. **The hierarchy is the substrate of conflict.** Without the tree,
   there is nothing for rivals to be siblings *of*; conflict and
   decomposition are the same picture seen from two angles.

These seven items are what the rest of this file tests against the
implementation plan.

## What the implementation already absorbs

### 1 — Inhibition as a first-class signal

[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md)
gives every manifest an optional `inhibits:` field, and
[`06-frames-polynemes-klines.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/06-frames-polynemes-klines.md)
lets polynemes carry both `excite:` and `inhibit:` weights against
named agents. The runtime then performs *graduated inhibition* in the
`criticize` tail
([`03-runtime-pipeline.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/03-runtime-pipeline.md)).
Figure 3-1's "loser is suppressed for this round" is therefore
representable for individual agencies.

### 2 — Family folders as the rough analogue of the figure's branches

`agencies/perception/`, `agencies/memory/`, `agencies/code/`,
`agencies/safety/`, `agencies/identity/`, `agencies/integration/`,
`agencies/assembly/`, `agencies/meta-admin/` mirror the figure's idea
that siblings share a parent. Rivalry among siblings — diff-skeptic
versus patch-imaginer, for instance — falls out of two members of the
same family making contradictory claims on the blackboard.

### 3 — Critics and censors as the formal resolution layer

The plan absorbs Minsky's "conflict is the default" claim by making
the `deliberate → criticize → censor → settle` chain mandatory in
[`02-workflow-design.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/02-workflow-design.md).
Every candidate action is expected to be objected to by *some* unit;
the settlement layer is the structural arbiter for which signal
survives.

### 4 — Settlement as the moment a conflict is closed

[`03-runtime-pipeline.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/03-runtime-pipeline.md)
hands the `settle` phase the full workspace and asks it to produce one
coherent outcome. This is the plan's version of "the louder rival
wins this round, and the result is written down so the next round
starts from there."

### 5 — Destruction is acknowledged as cheaper than construction

The presence of `revert-path-finder` as a required slot in the
`code-change` frame
([`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md),
[`07-policies-and-safety.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/07-policies-and-safety.md)),
and of `blast-radius-fear`, `soul-file-guardian`, and the
`workflow_mutation` / `soul_mutation` danger zones, reflects the
figure's reward asymmetry: a build is patient and reversible, a
wreck is fast and hard to undo, and the plan adds friction in
proportion.

## What the implementation does not yet take into account

### A — No top-level drives competing for the body

Figure 3-1's top row is EAT, PLAY, SLEEP. The plan has no analogue.
The runtime is single-tenant per stimulus: one issue or PR triggers
one run, and within that run there is no notion of *which top-level
agency owns the body right now*. There is no `agencies/drives/`
family, no resource that several drives bid for, and no scheduler
that decides between two drives that both want to run. The figure's
top tier is simply absent.

### B — Sibling rivalry is implicit, not declared

A diff-skeptic objecting to a patch-imaginer's diff is a rivalry in
practice, but the plan does not record that pairing anywhere. No
manifest field says "I am the structural opponent of
`agency.code.patch-imaginer`" the way WRECKER is the declared
opponent of BUILDER. Rivalries exist only as the side effect of two
agencies happening to write contradictory signals; they cannot be
queried, audited, or evolved as relationships in their own right.

### C — No WRECKER-shaped role

Every first-ship agency in
[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md)
is constructive or defensive: imaginer, implementer, skeptic,
revert-path-finder, guardian, censor. The catalogue contains no agent
whose *job* is to propose dismantling, removing, or simplifying — no
`agency.code.simplifier`, no `agency.assembly.pruner`. Minsky's
insistence that destruction is a legitimate child of construction is
present in the safety machinery but not in the active agency
catalogue.

### D — No shared-effector arbitration

In the figure, two rivals fight because they want the same hands. In
the plan, the only shared effector is `act.ts`, and the only thing
arbitrated there is the approval gate
([`07-policies-and-safety.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/07-policies-and-safety.md)).
There is no protocol by which two candidate actions on the same
artefact (two diffs to the same file, say) compete for the right to
become *the* action. The plan assumes the settlement layer has
already collapsed candidates to one; it does not describe the
collapse.

### E — Conflict is not first-class in the data model

[`09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md)
defines `signal`, `handoff`, `objection`, `candidate_action`,
`validation_plan`, and `validation_result`. None of them carries the
relationship "this signal is in direct conflict with that signal."
Objections attach to candidates, but two candidates that contradict
each other have no link between them. The conflict graph is therefore
not addressable by any critic or censor.

### F — No layered conflict in the agency hierarchy

Figure 3-1 shows conflict at three levels — drives, pastimes,
techniques. The plan has, at most, conflict at one level: members of
a family disagreeing inside one run. There is no recursive notion
that *families themselves* can be in conflict, because families are
folders rather than addressable units (the same gap recorded for
Figure 1-2). The figure's central image — conflict at *every* level —
is therefore representable at only the lowest level.

### G — The arbiter is named, the rivals are not

The plan names its arbiters precisely: `settle.ts`, the approval
gate, the censor stack, the suppressor list. It does not name the
*rivalry* those arbiters exist to mediate. A reader of the plan
learns how a winner is announced, but not who was running against
whom.

## Implication for the plan (no changes proposed here)

Figure 3-1 is the figure that asks *what is being arbitrated, and at
how many levels?* The implementation plan answers the second question
("one") and answers the first by inference rather than by declaration.
Conflict is treated as a property of the workflow, not as an object
the workflow operates on.

The gaps above are recorded here as analysis, not as a change
request. Any move to close them — a `rivals:` field on a manifest, an
`agencies/drives/` family, a `conflict` record in
[`09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/09-handoff-and-signal-schemas.md),
or a scheduler that arbitrates between candidates on the same
artefact — would touch the manifest schema in
[`05-agencies-critics-censors.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/05-agencies-critics-censors.md),
the activation protocol in
[`06-frames-polynemes-klines.md`](../../../../../FORGEJO-SOCIETY-IMPLEMENTATION/06-frames-polynemes-klines.md),
and the identity scopes in `THE-SOCIETY-OF-REPO/02-protocols/01-identity.md`,
and so falls under the "stop and ask" rules in
[`AGENTS.md`](../../../../../AGENTS.md) §12 and
[`CLAUDE.md`](../../../../../CLAUDE.md).
